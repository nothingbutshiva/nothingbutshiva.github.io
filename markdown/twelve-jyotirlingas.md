# The Twelve Jyotirlingas of Lord Shiva (द्वादश ज्योतिर्लिंग)

## Introduction

In the vast spiritual landscape of Sanātana Dharma, few pilgrimages carry as much reverence as the journey to the twelve Jyotirlingas — the radiant shrines where Lord Shiva is believed to have manifested as an infinite column of light (ज्योति). The word "Jyotirlinga" itself is a union of two Sanskrit terms: *jyoti* (ज्योति — light, radiance) and *linga* (लिंग — the sacred symbol of Shiva), together signifying the "linga of luminous light."

### The Origin — The Pillar of Endless Light

The Shiva Purāṇa (शिव पुराण) narrates the primordial legend that gives the Jyotirlingas their meaning. In the earliest age of creation, Lord Brahmā (the creator) and Lord Viṣṇu (the preserver) fell into a dispute over who among them was supreme. As their argument intensified and threatened cosmic order, Lord Shiva intervened by manifesting as an immeasurable column of fire — a blazing pillar of light (ज्योतिर्स्तम्भ) that stretched endlessly upward into the heavens and downward into the netherworlds.

Brahmā took the form of a swan (हंस) and flew upward to find the top, while Viṣṇu transformed into a boar (वराह) and burrowed downward to find the base. After ages of searching, neither could discover the extent of this cosmic pillar. Viṣṇu humbly admitted his inability, but Brahmā falsely claimed to have found the summit, citing the ketakī flower as his witness. Shiva, angered by the deceit, cursed Brahmā — declaring that Brahmā would not be widely worshipped — and cursed the ketakī flower to never be used in his worship. Shiva then revealed his true form, and both deities bowed in reverence.

The places where this infinite light pierced the earth are believed to be the Jyotirlingas. Though Shiva's radiance is omnipresent, tradition holds that twelve sites are especially sacred — places where devotees can experience the living presence of that original column of divine fire. The Shiva Purāṇa declares:

> **द्वादशैतानि नामानि प्रातरुत्थाय यः पठेत् ।**
> **सर्वपापैर्विनिर्मुक्तः सर्वसिद्धिफलं लभेत् ॥**
>
> *"One who recites these twelve names upon waking at dawn is freed from all sins and attains the fruit of all accomplishments."*

The traditional śloka enumerating all twelve Jyotirlingas, widely recited by pilgrims, is:

> **सौराष्ट्रे सोमनाथं च श्रीशैले मल्लिकार्जुनम् ।**
> **उज्जयिन्यां महाकालमोङ्कारममलेश्वरम् ॥**
> **परल्यां वैद्यनाथं च डाकिन्यां भीमशङ्करम् ।**
> **सेतुबन्धे तु रामेशं नागेशं दारुकावने ॥**
> **वाराणस्यां तु विश्वेशं त्र्यम्बकं गौतमीतटे ।**
> **हिमालये तु केदारं घृष्णेशं च शिवालये ॥**
> **एतानि ज्योतिर्लिङ्गानि सायं प्रातः पठेन्नरः ।**
> **सप्तजन्मकृतं पापं स्मरणेन विनश्यति ॥**

## 1. Somnath (सोमनाथ) — Gujarat

**Location:** Prabhās Paṭan, near Verāval, Saurāṣṭra, Gujarat — on the western coast of India, overlooking the Arabian Sea.

### Legend

The Skanda Purāṇa and Shiva Purāṇa recount that Soma (the Moon god, चन्द्र) was married to the twenty-seven daughters of Dakṣa Prajāpati, each representing a lunar constellation (nakṣatra). However, Soma was excessively devoted to Rohiṇī and neglected the other wives. Enraged by this partiality, Dakṣa cursed Soma to lose his lustre and wane until he vanished entirely.

Stricken with a wasting disease that drained his radiance, Soma — on the advice of Brahmā — came to Prabhāsa Kṣetra with his wives and performed intense penance to Lord Shiva, worshipping a Linga on the banks of the sacred river Sarasvatī. Pleased by Soma's devotion, Shiva appeared and partially lifted the curse: the Moon would wax and wane in an eternal cycle rather than perish completely. Grateful beyond measure, Soma established the first shrine here, and Shiva chose to reside eternally at this spot as Somnātha — "the Lord of Soma (the Moon)."

### Significance

Somnath holds the honour of being the first among the twelve Jyotirlingas as enumerated in the traditional śloka (*"Saurāṣṭre Somnāthaṁ ca…"*). It symbolises restoration, renewal, and divine compassion. The waxing and waning of the moon itself stands as a perpetual reminder of Shiva's grace.

### Key Features

The present temple, reconstructed in the Chālukya style of architecture, stands majestically on the shore. The temple's *bāṇa-stambha* (arrow pillar) on the sea-protection wall bears an inscription declaring that there is no land between this point and the South Pole — a testament to its coastal geography. The temple complex also includes the ancient triveni saṅgama (confluence) of the rivers Hiraṇyā, Kapilā, and the legendary Sarasvatī.

## 2. Mallikarjuna (मल्लिकार्जुन) — Andhra Pradesh

**Location:** Śrīśailam, Kurnool district, Andhra Pradesh — atop the Nallamala Hills, on the banks of the river Kṛṣṇā.

### Legend

According to the Shiva Purāṇa, when Lord Gaṇeśa and Kārttikeya (Subrahmaṇya) competed to circumambulate the universe first, Gaṇeśa wisely circled his parents — Shiva and Pārvatī — declaring them to be the whole universe. Victorious, Gaṇeśa was married first. Kārttikeya, hurt and angry, departed for the Krauñca mountain in solitude. Heartbroken, Shiva and Pārvatī followed. Shiva manifested as Mallikārjuna at Śrīśailam to remain close to their son. *Mallikā* means jasmine (symbolising Pārvatī) and *Arjuna* is another name for Shiva — together representing the inseparable divine couple.

### Significance

Śrīśailam is revered as both a Jyotirlinga and a Śakti Pīṭha (seat of the Goddess as Bhramarāmbā). This dual sanctity makes it extraordinarily sacred. The Skanda Purāṇa extols it as *"Śrīśaila-śikhare devi, dṛśyate bharatī-bhuvam"* — from the peak of Śrīśailam, the whole of Bhāratavarṣa can be perceived spiritually.

### Key Features

The temple is an ancient Dravidian-style complex set amidst dense forests. The Śrīśailam dam (Srisailam) on the river Kṛṣṇā, the Pātālagaṅgā steps leading down to the river, and the Śikhareśvara temple on the hilltop are all notable pilgrimage points.

## 3. Mahakaleshwar (महाकालेश्वर) — Madhya Pradesh

**Location:** Ujjain (ancient Avantikā), Madhya Pradesh — on the banks of the river Kṣiprā.

### Legend

The Shiva Purāṇa narrates that a demon named Dūṣaṇa besieged the city of Avantī and tormented its righteous inhabitants. A devout Brāhmaṇa and his family, along with other devotees, prayed fervently to Lord Shiva. Moved by their devotion, Shiva manifested from the earth in a terrifying form as Mahākāla — "the Great Lord of Time" — and annihilated the demon with a single roar. At the devotees' request, Shiva chose to dwell there permanently as the Mahākāleśvara Jyotirlinga.

### Significance

Mahākāleśvara is unique among the twelve Jyotirlingas because its Linga is regarded as *svayambhū* (self-manifested) and *dakṣiṇāmukhī* (south-facing). In the Śaiva Tantra tradition, the south-facing aspect signifies Shiva as the conqueror of death. Ujjain itself, one of the seven sacred cities (Sapta Purī) of India, is the site of the Kumbha Melā (Siṁhastha) held every twelve years.

### Key Features

The famed *Bhasm Ārtī* (भस्म आरती) performed before dawn — in which the Linga is anointed with sacred ash from cremation grounds — is a singular ritual found only here. The temple has three levels, with the Mahākāla Linga enshrined in the lowest sanctum (garbhagṛha) below ground.

## 4. Omkareshwar (ओंकारेश्वर) — Madhya Pradesh

**Location:** Māndhātā island, on the river Narmadā, Khandwa district, Madhya Pradesh.

### Legend

Scriptures relate that the Vindhya mountain, filled with pride, once performed intense penance to Lord Shiva to become the greatest among mountains. Shiva, pleased by this austerity, manifested as Oṁkāreśvara. Some accounts from the Shiva Purāṇa also describe the Devas and Asuras jointly worshipping Shiva here with the sacred syllable Oṁ (ॐ), whereupon Shiva appeared in two forms — Oṁkāreśvara and Amarēśvara (sometimes counted together or separately depending on tradition).

### Significance

The island of Māndhātā itself is shaped like the sacred syllable ॐ when viewed from above — a natural geographic formation considered profoundly auspicious. Oṁkāreśvara represents the primordial vibration from which all creation emerged.

### Key Features

Two major temples sit on the island: Oṁkāreśvara and Amarēśvara. Pilgrims traditionally perform a *parikramā* (circumambulation) of the entire island. The confluence of the Narmadā and Kāverī rivers near the island is considered sacred for ancestral rites.

## 5. Kedarnath (केदारनाथ) — Uttarakhand

**Location:** Kedārnāth, Rudraprayāg district, Uttarakhand — in the Garhwal Himālayas, at an altitude of approximately 3,583 metres, near the head of the Mandākinī river.

### Legend

The Mahābhārata and Skanda Purāṇa describe that after the great war of Kurukṣetra, the five Pāṇḍava brothers sought Lord Shiva's forgiveness for the sin of slaying their own kinsmen. Shiva, unwilling to meet them, disguised himself as a bull (Nandī) and hid among the cattle in the Himālayas. Bhīma recognised the divine bull. When seized, the bull sank into the earth. Its hump remained at Kedārnāth, while other body parts appeared at four other locations — together forming the Pañca Kedāra (five Kedāra shrines). Shiva, impressed by their persistence and devotion, agreed to absolve the Pāṇḍavas and reside here eternally.

### Significance

Kedārnāth is the northernmost Jyotirlinga, set amidst perpetual snow and glaciers, embodying Shiva's aspect as the ascetic lord of the mountains (Girīśa). It is part of the sacred Chār Dhām pilgrimage circuit of Uttarakhand. The temple remains open only from the auspicious day of Akṣaya Tṛtīyā (April/May) to Kārtik Pūrṇimā (November).

### Key Features

The ancient stone temple, attributed to the Pāṇḍavas and later restored by Ādi Śaṅkarācārya in the 8th century, features a distinctive conical Linga representing the hump of the bull. Behind the temple stands the imposing Kedārnāth peak. The samādhi of Ādi Śaṅkarācārya is located nearby.

## 6. Bhimashankar (भीमाशंकर) — Maharashtra

**Location:** Bhīmāśaṅkar, Pune district, Maharashtra — in the Sahyādri range of the Western Ghats, the source of the river Bhīmā.

### Legend

The Shiva Purāṇa tells of the demon Bhīma, son of Kumbhakarṇa, who performed severe austerities and gained great power. Filled with arrogance, he waged war against the Devas and captured Kāmarupēśvara, a devout king and ardent Shiva-bhakta. When Bhīma tried to destroy the Shiva Linga worshipped by the king, Lord Shiva emerged in his fierce form and destroyed the demon in battle. The sweat (perspiration) that poured from Shiva's body during the fierce combat formed the river Bhīmā. At the entreaty of the Devas, Shiva agreed to reside at this place as Bhīmāśaṅkara.

### Significance

Bhīmāśaṅkar represents Shiva as the protector of his devotees — the one who manifests to defend the faithful against oppression. The surrounding Bhīmāśaṅkar Wildlife Sanctuary is home to the Indian giant squirrel and dense tropical forests, lending the pilgrimage a profound sense of nature's sanctity.

### Key Features

The temple is built in the Nāgara style of architecture. A massive bell (donated during the Peśvā era) hangs in the temple complex. The trek to the temple through lush forest and waterfalls is itself a meditative journey.

## 7. Kashi Vishwanath (काशी विश्वनाथ) — Uttar Pradesh

**Location:** Vārāṇasī (Kāśī / Banāras), Uttar Pradesh — on the western bank of the river Gaṅgā.

### Legend

Kāśī occupies a singular position in Hindu cosmology. The Kāśī Khaṇḍa of the Skanda Purāṇa declares that Kāśī is not situated on the earth at all — it rests on Shiva's trident (triśūla). When the universe dissolves at the end of each cosmic cycle (pralaya), Kāśī alone is preserved, lifted above the floodwaters on Shiva's trident. Here, Shiva himself whispers the *tāraka mantra* (the liberating name of Rāma) into the ear of every being who dies within the city, granting instant mokṣa (liberation).

### Significance

Viśvanātha — "Lord of the Universe" — is the presiding deity of the most ancient continuously inhabited city in the world. This Jyotirlinga is considered supreme for those seeking spiritual liberation. The Liṅga Purāṇa states: *"Kāśyāṁ hi kāśate kāśī, kāśī sarvaprakāśikā"* — Kāśī illuminates all; Kāśī is the light of all lights.

### Key Features

The temple, situated in the narrow lanes (galīs) of the old city, stands adjacent to the Jñānavāpī well, whose waters are said to contain the essence of spiritual wisdom. The daily Gaṅgā Ārtī at the nearby Daśāśvamedha Ghāṭ is one of the most spiritually moving rituals in all of India. The temple complex was expanded and renovated as part of the Kāśī Viśvanātha Dham corridor.

## 8. Trimbakeshwar (त्र्यंबकेश्वर) — Maharashtra

**Location:** Trimbak, Nāsik district, Maharashtra — at the source of the river Godāvarī, near the Brahmagiri hills.

### Legend

According to the Shiva Purāṇa, the sage Gautama Ṛṣi, after being falsely accused of *go-hatyā* (cow-slaughter) through the trickery of other envious sages, performed great penance to Shiva. Pleased, Shiva released the river Gaṅgā (here called Godāvarī or Gautamī Gaṅgā) from his matted locks to purify Gautama and his hermitage. At Gautama's request, Shiva and Pārvatī agreed to dwell eternally at Trimbak.

### Significance

Tryambakeśvara — "the three-eyed Lord" — is unique because the Linga here has three faces representing Brahmā, Viṣṇu, and Rudra (the Trimūrti). The temple is the origin of the sacred river Godāvarī, the longest river in peninsular India. The Siṁhastha Kumbha Melā is held at nearby Nāsik every twelve years.

### Key Features

The temple, built entirely of basalt in the Nāgara style, features intricate carvings and a spacious courtyard. The Kuśāvarta Tīrtha (sacred tank considered the true source of the Godāvarī) lies within the temple complex. The Brahmagiri hill behind the temple can be climbed as part of a sacred parikramā.

## 9. Vaidyanath (वैद्यनाथ) — Jharkhand

**Location:** Devghar (Vaidyanāth Dhām), Jharkhand.

### Legend

The Shiva Purāṇa recounts that Rāvaṇa, the mighty king of Laṅkā and an ardent devotee of Shiva, performed fierce austerities on Mount Kailāsa. To demonstrate his supreme devotion, he offered his ten heads one by one to Shiva. Moved by this extraordinary sacrifice, Shiva appeared and healed (vaidya — physician) Rāvaṇa's self-inflicted wounds, restoring all ten heads. Shiva then granted him a boon and became known as Vaidyanātha — "the Lord who heals." Rāvaṇa wished to carry Shiva's Linga to Laṅkā, but through divine intervention (the Linga was set down at this spot and became rooted to the earth), Shiva remained at Devghar.

### Significance

Vaidyanātha embodies Shiva as the divine physician and healer — the ultimate remedy for the afflictions of body, mind, and soul. Devghar literally means "abode of the gods." Devotees seeking relief from illness and disease especially revere this Jyotirlinga.

### Key Features

The temple complex houses twenty-two other temples within its compound. The annual Śrāvaṇa Melā (July–August) sees one of the largest religious gatherings in India, when millions of *kāṁvaḍiyās* (devotees carrying sacred Gaṅgā water) walk over a hundred kilometres from Sultānganj to offer water to the Linga here.

## 10. Nageshwar (नागेश्वर) — Gujarat

**Location:** Near Dwārakā, Gujarat — situated between Dwārakā and the island of Beyt Dwārakā on the coast of Saurāṣṭra.

### Legend

The Shiva Purāṇa narrates the story of a devout Shiva-bhakta named Supriya (in some versions, a merchant named Nāmdev or Daaruka). He was captured by a demon named Dāruka (and the demoness Dārukā), who imprisoned devotees in their underwater city of Dārukāvana. Even in captivity, Supriya continued to chant the Pañcākṣarī Mantra (*Oṁ Namaḥ Śivāya*) and inspired fellow prisoners to worship Shiva. Enraged, the demon tried to kill him. At that moment, Lord Shiva manifested, vanquished the demon with his divine weapon (the Pāśupatāstra), and liberated all the devotees. Shiva then chose to reside at this place as Nāgeśvara — "the Lord of Serpents" — for the protection of all devotees.

### Significance

Nāgeśvara signifies Shiva as the supreme protector from all poisons — both literal (serpent venom) and metaphorical (the venom of worldly suffering). The traditional śloka places Nāgeśvara in *Dārukāvane* (the forest of Dāruka).

### Key Features

A towering statue of Shiva in meditation (one of the tallest in the region) is a landmark at the temple entrance. The temple's proximity to Dwārakā — one of the four Dhāms sacred to Viṣṇu — makes it part of a unique dual-pilgrimage for devotees of both Shiva and Viṣṇu.

## 11. Rameshwaram (रामेश्वरम्) — Tamil Nadu

**Location:** Rāmeśvaram island, Rāmanāthapuram district, Tamil Nadu — connected to mainland India by the Pambān Bridge, near the southeastern tip of the subcontinent.

### Legend

The Rāmāyaṇa and Skanda Purāṇa narrate that Lord Rāma, before building the Setu (bridge) to Laṅkā to rescue Sītā, wished to worship Lord Shiva to atone for the sin of killing Rāvaṇa (a Brāhmaṇa by birth). He sent Hanumān to Mount Kailāsa to bring a Linga, but when Hanumān was delayed, Sītā fashioned a Linga from the sand of the seashore so that the auspicious hour would not pass. Rāma worshipped this sand Linga. When Hanumān returned with the Kailāsa Linga, Rāma, to honour Hanumān's devotion, installed it alongside the original, declaring that Hanumān's Linga should be worshipped first. The original sand Linga worshipped by Rāma became the Rāmanāthasvāmī Jyotirlinga.

### Significance

Rāmeśvaram uniquely connects the two great traditions of Śaivism and Vaiṣṇavism — here, Viṣṇu's avatāra (Rāma) worships Shiva. It is one of the four Dhāms (Chār Dhām) of India and is considered the southern counterpart of Kāśī. Tradition holds that a pilgrimage to Kāśī is incomplete without visiting Rāmeśvaram, and vice versa.

### Key Features

The Rāmanāthasvāmī Temple is renowned for its magnificent corridors — among the longest in the world — with ornately carved granite pillars. The temple has 22 sacred wells (tīrthas) within its complex; bathing in each is considered purifying. The Agni Tīrtham, on the seashore adjacent to the temple, is the site where pilgrims perform rituals for ancestors.

## 12. Grishneshwar (घृष्णेश्वर) — Maharashtra

**Location:** Near Ellora (Verūl), Aurangabad district, Maharashtra — close to the renowned Ellora Caves.

### Legend

The Shiva Purāṇa recounts the story of a devout woman named Kusumā (in some recensions, Ghṛṣṇā). She was deeply devoted to Shiva and would daily fashion 101 Lingas from clay, worship them, and immerse them in a nearby lake. Her stepson was murdered through the jealousy of her co-wife. Kusumā, though grief-stricken, continued her worship with unwavering faith. Moved by her extraordinary devotion and steadfastness even in the face of tragedy, Lord Shiva appeared before her, restored her son to life, and offered to destroy the wicked co-wife. Kusumā, embodying compassion, asked Shiva to forgive the wrongdoer. Shiva, moved by her mercy, chose to reside there eternally as Ghṛṣṇeśvara — the last of the twelve Jyotirlingas.

### Significance

Ghṛṣṇeśvara represents the culmination of the Jyotirlinga pilgrimage and exemplifies the power of unwavering devotion (अनन्य भक्ति) coupled with forgiveness. It is the smallest of the twelve Jyotirlinga temples in physical size but immense in spiritual potency.

### Key Features

The temple, rebuilt in the 18th century by Queen Ahilyābāī Holkar of Indore (who also restored several other Jyotirlinga temples), is a fine example of Marāṭhā architecture with red rock construction and elegant carvings. Its proximity to the UNESCO World Heritage Site of Ellora Caves — which contain the awe-inspiring Kailāsa Temple (Cave 16) carved from a single rock — makes this a destination of both spiritual and cultural significance.

## The Sacred Verse of the Twelve Jyotirlingas — A Recapitulation

The complete traditional śloka for daily recitation:

> **सौराष्ट्रे सोमनाथं च श्रीशैले मल्लिकार्जुनम् ।**
> **उज्जयिन्यां महाकालमोङ्कारममलेश्वरम् ॥**
> **परल्यां वैद्यनाथं च डाकिन्यां भीमशङ्करम् ।**
> **सेतुबन्धे तु रामेशं नागेशं दारुकावने ॥**
> **वाराणस्यां तु विश्वेशं त्र्यम्बकं गौतमीतटे ।**
> **हिमालये तु केदारं घृष्णेशं च शिवालये ॥**
> **एतानि ज्योतिर्लिङ्गानि सायं प्रातः पठेन्नरः ।**
> **सप्तजन्मकृतं पापं स्मरणेन विनश्यति ॥**

*"In Saurāṣṭra, Somnāth; on Śrīśaila, Mallikārjuna; in Ujjain, Mahākāla; and Oṁkāra Amalēśvara. In Paralī, Vaidyanātha; in Ḍākinī, Bhīmāśaṅkara; at Setubandha, Rāmeśa; and Nāgeśa in Dārukāvana. In Vārāṇasī, Viśveśa; Tryambaka on the banks of the Gautamī. In the Himālayas, Kedāra; and Ghṛṣṇeśa in the abode of Shiva. One who recites these Jyotirlingas at dusk and dawn — the sins of seven lifetimes are destroyed by that remembrance."*

## How to Visit — A Pilgrimage Guide

### Planning the Yātrā

Visiting all twelve Jyotirlingas is considered one of the most meritorious pilgrimages in Sanātana Dharma. While no fixed order is prescribed, many devotees follow the sequence in the traditional śloka. A complete circuit typically takes 15–20 days by road and rail. Organised pilgrimage tours (both government and private) are widely available.

### Geographical Grouping for Practical Travel

- **Western India (Gujarat):** Somnath and Nageshwar (near Dwārakā) — can be combined in 2–3 days.
- **Central India (Madhya Pradesh):** Mahakaleshwar (Ujjain) and Omkareshwar — 2 days, as they are roughly 150 km apart.
- **Maharashtra (Western Ghats):** Bhimashankar, Trimbakeshwar, and Grishneshwar — 3–4 days; all accessible from Pune or Nāsik.
- **South India:** Mallikarjuna (Śrīśailam, Andhra Pradesh) and Rameshwaram (Tamil Nadu) — 3–4 days.
- **North India:** Kashi Vishwanath (Vārāṇasī, Uttar Pradesh) and Kedarnath (Uttarakhand) — 4–5 days. Kedārnāth is accessible only from May to November.
- **East India:** Vaidyanath (Devghar, Jharkhand) — 1–2 days.

### General Tips

- **Best Season:** October to March for most temples. Kedārnāth is only open May–November. Monsoon (July–September) enhances the beauty of Bhimashankar and Trimbakeshwar but may cause travel disruptions.
- **Dress Code:** Modest traditional attire is expected inside temple premises.
- **Darśan Timings:** Temples generally open between 4:00–5:00 AM and close by 9:00–10:00 PM, with a midday break. Special ārtīs (such as the Bhasm Ārtī at Ujjain) require very early arrival.
- **Accessibility:** Most temples are well-connected by road and rail. Kedārnāth requires a 16 km trek from Gaurikuṇḍ (pony, palki, and helicopter services are available).
- **Respectful Conduct:** Photography is restricted inside most sanctum areas. Maintain silence and reverence within temple precincts.

### The Spiritual Fruit

The Shiva Purāṇa assures that even the sincere intention (*saṅkalpa*) to undertake the Dvādaśa Jyotirlinga Yātrā begins to purify the devotee. Each shrine is a doorway to the infinite — a place where the veil between the mortal world and the divine grows thin, and where the eternal light of Mahadeva continues to blaze, as it has since the beginning of time.

> **ॐ नमः शिवाय**
> *Oṁ Namaḥ Śivāya*
